Reflections

Structured as two parts (ENG2003 experience + personal growth) with examples and links to course concepts.

Part 1

ENG2003 Experience

ENG2003 felt different from my technical courses because communication, not equations, was the center of the work.

My overall experience was mixed but valuable. Some parts of the course were engaging, especially interactive moments like Kahoot sessions, where I retained key ideas more effectively than in passive lecture sections. Tutorials were generally positive as well, and working with classmates made many in-class exercises smoother and more collaborative.

The most useful component for me was Term Project 1 (technical report writing). It was my first time building a full technical report with formal structure and IEEE citations. That process taught me how to move from opinion-based writing to evidence-based technical communication. I had to think carefully about claim quality, source credibility, and flow between sections, which made this assignment stand out from shorter writing tasks.

The group presentation assignment was also useful because it required communication in two layers: internal team coordination and external audience delivery. We had to align our sections, resolve differing work styles, and still produce one coherent final message. This directly connected with the communication axiom that meaning is created not only by what is said, but by how messages are interpreted in context.

In terms of expectations, the course was somewhat better than I expected. I anticipated mostly repetitive writing tasks, but several activities were more interactive and practical than expected. At the same time, some tasks felt routine for my background. This made me realize that communication training has different value depending on prior experience: foundational for some, refinement-focused for others.

Part 2

Personal Growth

Before ENG2003, I already had a strong communication base from hackathons, team projects, and technical collaboration.

After the course, my biggest change was not in everyday conversation, but in awareness. I now pay closer attention to structure, audience interpretation, and communication purpose before I start writing or presenting. Even when I do not consciously list all 7 C's while speaking, I now audit my communication against them after drafting: clarity, coherence, and completeness have become stronger checkpoints in my process.

The skill I improved most was technical writing discipline. The report assignment pushed me to communicate ideas with stronger evidence and better organization. I also improved in asynchronous communication (video interview and recorded presentation), though this remains my weakest area because speaking to a camera still feels less natural than live interaction.

The communication skill I still need to improve most is concise delivery under pressure. I sometimes over-explain when I want to be precise. Going forward, I want to strengthen the balance between technical depth and brevity, especially for interviews, project demos, and stakeholder updates.

This course will influence how I approach future engineering courses and practice in three ways. First, I will structure reports and design rationales more intentionally so teammates can understand decisions quickly. Second, I will communicate role expectations and deadlines earlier in team settings to reduce ambiguity. Third, I will continue practicing camera-based communication because engineering increasingly requires remote demos and asynchronous project updates.

Overall, ENG2003 reinforced that communication is not separate from engineering work. It is part of engineering quality: if an idea is technically sound but poorly communicated, collaboration slows and impact drops.
